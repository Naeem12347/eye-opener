Identity formation is a central task of adolescence, as teenagers explore and define their sense of self. This process involves the integration of various aspects of identity, including personal values, beliefs, and aspirations. Erik Erikson’s theory of psychological development emphasizes the importance of achieving a coherent sense of identity, viewing adolescence as a critical period for resolving identity versus role confusion. During this time, teenagers engage in self-reflection and experimentation, often questioning established norms and seeking autonomy. This exploration is not without its challenges; the quest for identity can lead to a sense of uncertainty and vulnerability. Adolescents may experience fluctuations in self-esteem and confidence as they navigate these complex waters. However this period of exploration is also a time of immense potential, allowing teenagers to develop a strong sense of self and purpose. Emotional development is another significant aspect of teenage life, characterised by heightened emotional intensity and volatility. Hormonal changes during puberty contribute to emotional fluctuations, impacting mood regulation and interpersonal relationships. Research by Steinberg(2014) suggests that the adolescent brain undergoes significant changes that affect emotional processing and decision-making. This period of emotional upheaval can lead to conflicts with parents and peers, as teenagers assert their independence and grapple with new found emotions. Despite the turbulence, adolescence is also a time of emotional maturation. Teenagers develop greater empathy and emotional intelligence, learning to manage their emotions and navigate complex social situations. These emotional skills are crucial for successful adult functioning, underscoring the importance of adolescence as a period of emotional growth and development. Building on the exploration of identity and emotional development, it becomes imperative to consider the role of external influences in shaping teenage experiences. Environmental factors, such as family dynamics and societal expectations, play a significant role in influencing adolescents’ development. Understanding these influences provides a holistic view of teenage life, highlighting the interconnectedness of internal and external factors
